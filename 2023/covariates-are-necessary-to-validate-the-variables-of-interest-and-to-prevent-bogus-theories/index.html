Covariates are necessary to validate the variables of interest and to prevent bogus theories

2023 research methods, statistics

The need for covariates in statistical analyses is twofold. The first need is purely statistical and the second reason is academic.

First, covariates are required when the variable(s) of interest in a study are connected to, and affected by, a set of ancillary variables (Bottini et al., 2022; Elze et al., 2017; Sassenhagen & Alday, 2016). This complex scenario is the most common one due to the multivariate, dynamic, interactive nature of the real world.

Second, covariates are necessary to prevent the development of bogus, redundant theories. Academics are strongly rewarded for developing theories and models. As we know, wherever there are strong rewards, there are serious risks. An academic could—consciously or not—produce a theory/model that is too closely related to an existing theory. So closely related are these theories that the second version hardly warrants a name of its own. In such a scenario, covariates are useful and indeed necessary to vet the unique nature of the second version. The first and the second versions must be tested in the same model. The variables corresponding to the first version can be construed as covariates, which can help evaluate the degree of separation between the original version of a theory and any subsequent approximations.

The perverted use of covariates (Stefan & Schönbrodt, 2023)—frequent though it may be—is completely orthogonal to the correct usage of covariates, in the same way that a stethoscope can be used for good or for bad purposes. It would be poorly informed and misleading to conflate the correct and the incorrect uses of a method or a tool.

After doing a research master's, I became a PhD student and graduate teaching assistant in Psychology at Lancaster University, where I investigated how conceptual processing—that is, the comprehension of the meaning of words—is supported by linguistic and sensorimotor brain systems, and how research on this topic is influenced by methodological aspects such as the operationalisation of variables and the sample size of experiments. Currently, I am a postdoctoral fellow at UiT The Arctic University of Norway, where I am investigating the behavioural and neural underpinnings of multilingualism. Throughout my research, I have used methods such as behavioural and electroencephalographic experiments, corpus analysis, statistics and programming. Research materials at https://osf.io/25u3x.
