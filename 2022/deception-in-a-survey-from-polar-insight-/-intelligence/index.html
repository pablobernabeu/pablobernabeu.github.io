Deception in a survey from Polar Insight / Intelligence

2022

I recommend extreme caution when receiving an invitation from Polar Insight (also dubbed ‘Polar Intelligence’) to complete a survey. When I received one, it ended up in a deceptive change of the terms. The deception emerged after two emails exchanged with the company’s managing director, James Tattersfield, and an employee, Emily Hopewell. Specifically, the director offered me to complete two tasks in exchange for a $125 USD voucher. The two tasks were the completion of a survey (‘short form’, in the director’s words) and the participation in a one-hour-long interview. Once the company received the survey completed, they changed the terms: ‘we have had a high volume of responses to our survey, so unfortunately, not everyone will be selected on this occasion’.


It all began when I received the following email from the managing director, James Tattersfield.

From: James Tattersfield james@polarintelligence.com Sent: Thursday, January 27, 2022 12:33:32 PM To: De Juan Bernabeu, Pablo p.bernabeu@lancaster.ac.uk Subject: [External] Research Interview Request

This email originated outside the University. Check before clicking links or attachments.

Hi Pablo,

I’m emailing you to discuss your participation in a global research study that my organisation (Polar Insight, a research firm based in London) is conducting to better understand how academic researchers are discovering and accessing scholarly articles outside university campus.

At this point in the project, we are particularly interested in hearing from those who have completed - or are currently completing - a PhD or post-doctorate degree.

My research team has identified you as a potential respondent and asked me to reach out to see whether you may be interested in taking part in one of our online, paid research interviews in February. Does this sound like something you would be interested in?

Details:

Format: Online Video Interview Time required: 60 minutes When: February 2022 Prep. required: None Reward for taking part: $125 USD (via our gifting partner, Tremendous)

Next steps:

  1. If you’d like to take part, please reconfirm your suitability using this short form: https://polarinsight.typeform.com/res-dis

  2. Once we receive your answers, myself or a member of the team will be in contact to get you scheduled in.

  3. After the interview is completed, we’ll be in touch to process your reward.

If you have any questions, please do feel free to reply to this email and I’ll do my best to answer them.

Thanks,

James

My rating for such an email was commensurate with the deception that had been uncovered. I replied:

From: De Juan Bernabeu, Pablo p.bernabeu@lancaster.ac.uk Sent: 01 February 2022 13:46 To: Emily Hopewell james@polarintelligence.com Subject: Re: [External] Research Interview Request

Hi Emily,

I am surprised. Nowhere in the email I received from the CEO of Polar Intelligence was there an indication of a selection procedure. On the contrary, the email indicated:

Next steps:

  1. If you’d like to take part, please reconfirm your suitability using this short form: https://polarinsight.typeform.com/res-dis

  2. Once we receive your answers, myself or a member of the team will be in contact to get you scheduled in.

  3. After the interview is completed, we’ll be in touch to process your reward.

I trust that the procedure outlined initially will be followed to ensure that respondents don’t feel deceived.

Best regards, Pablo


I followed this up with an email to the managing director. Receiving no reply from either person, I reached the director on Linkedin, and was met with evasive questions and with the reply that the voucher was bound to the interview only. Very cunningly, in this way, the director sidelined the fact that the interview was supposed to follow the initial survey, according to his email. Alas, the initial terms had been changed only after the company had received the data from the survey.

Next, I wrote a post about this deceptive activity on Linkedin, and then the managing director did email me as I had requested:

Changes of the terms and ethics

Changes of the terms such as the one performed by this company are unethical, as they happen after the engagement has commenced, and they are disadvantageous to respondents. Instead, an example of an ethical change of the terms is to add benefits after respondents have been engaged. Of course, if the first email from this company had truthfully provided the information later disclosed by the employee, the company would not have received nearly as many surveys completed.

Ethics is important in the distribution of surveys, and it is not difficult.
